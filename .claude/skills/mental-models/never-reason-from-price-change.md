# Never Reason from a Price Change

A price change is an outcome, not a cause. Reasoning from a price change means treating the symptom as the explanation — and getting the downstream effects wrong because you ignored why the price moved.

## The Error

"Oil prices rose, so consumers will spend less on other goods." This sounds reasonable but skips the crucial step: *why* did oil prices rise? The cause determines the consequences:

- **Supply disruption** (war, embargo) — less oil available. Yes, consumers spend less elsewhere. The naive conclusion happens to be right, but only by accident.
- **Demand surge** (economic boom) — consumers are richer. They're spending MORE on everything, including oil. The naive conclusion is backwards.
- **Speculation/expectations** — prices may revert. Consumers barely change behavior.

Same price change, completely different downstream effects. The price tells you an equilibrium shifted; it doesn't tell you which curve moved or why.

## Source

Scott Sumner, *The Money Illusion*. Sumner's canonical example: "interest rates fell, so monetary policy must be loose." But rates fall for different reasons — loose money (liquidity effect) vs. weak economy (income effect) vs. low expected inflation (Fisher effect). The Fed cutting rates into a recession isn't "loose" policy if the natural rate fell further. Reasoning from the rate change leads you astray.

## How to Apply

When you catch yourself reasoning "X price went up/down, therefore Y":

1. **Stop.** Ask: why did the price change?
2. **Identify the cause** — supply shift, demand shift, expectations, policy, structural change
3. **Reason from the cause**, not the price. The cause determines the downstream effects.
4. **Check direction** — would a different cause of the same price change produce the opposite conclusion? If yes, you were reasoning from the price change.

## Common Traps

- "Housing prices fell, so people feel poorer and spend less" — ignores that falling prices make housing more affordable for buyers
- "The dollar weakened, so exports will boom" — depends on whether the dollar fell due to loose monetary policy (maybe) or declining productivity (probably not)
- "Wages rose, so companies will hire fewer workers" — if wages rose because of productivity gains, companies may hire more
- "Stock market crashed, so recession is coming" — the crash may reflect a recession already priced in, or a valuation correction with no real-economy cause
